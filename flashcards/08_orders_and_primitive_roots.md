# Number Theory — Orders and Primitive Roots

## 8.1 The Order of an Element

C: For $\gcd(a, n) = 1$, the [order] of $a$ modulo $n$, written $\text{ord}_n(a)$, is the smallest positive integer $k$ with $a^k \equiv 1 \pmod n$.

Q: Why is the order well-defined?
A: Because Euler's theorem guarantees $a^{\varphi(n)} \equiv 1 \pmod n$, so the set $\{k \geq 1 : a^k \equiv 1 \pmod n\}$ is nonempty. Well-ordering gives a least element — the order. The order is the period of the sequence $a, a^2, a^3, \dots$ mod $n$.

Q: What does it mean algorithmically to compute an order?
A: Compute $a^k \bmod n$ for $k = 1, 2, \dots$ until the result is $1$. Naive approach is $O(\text{ord}_n(a))$ multiplications. Better: check $k = d$ for each divisor $d$ of $\varphi(n)$ (since the order divides $\varphi(n)$ — see §8.2) — fast when $\varphi(n)$'s factorization is known.

## 8.2 Order Divides $\varphi(n)$

Q: Why does $\text{ord}_n(a) \mid \varphi(n)$?
A: Write $\varphi(n) = q \cdot \text{ord}_n(a) + r$ with $0 \leq r < \text{ord}_n(a)$. Then $1 \equiv a^{\varphi(n)} = a^{q \cdot \text{ord} + r} = (a^{\text{ord}})^q \cdot a^r = a^r \pmod n$. If $r > 0$, this contradicts minimality of $\text{ord}$. So $r = 0$, meaning $\text{ord}_n(a) \mid \varphi(n)$. Group-theoretic: order of an element divides group order (Lagrange's theorem).

Q: Why does the same argument show $\text{ord}_n(a) \mid k$ whenever $a^k \equiv 1 \pmod n$?
A: Divide $k$ by $\text{ord}_n(a)$: $k = q \cdot \text{ord} + r$ with $0 \leq r < \text{ord}$. If $a^k \equiv 1$, then $a^r \equiv 1$ too; minimality forces $r = 0$. So "the exponents that give $1$" are exactly the multiples of $\text{ord}_n(a)$ — the order generates this ideal.

## 8.3 Primitive Roots

C: An integer $g$ coprime to $n$ is a [primitive root modulo $n$] if $\text{ord}_n(g) = \varphi(n)$ — that is, the powers $g^1, g^2, \dots, g^{\varphi(n)}$ cycle through every element of $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$.

Q: What does "primitive root exists mod $n$" mean algebraically?
A: It means $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$ is a CYCLIC group (generated by a single element). The primitive root is a generator. Cyclicity is a strong structural property making the unit group "one-dimensional" — every element is $g^k$ for some exponent.

## 8.4 Which Moduli Admit Primitive Roots?

Q: For which $n$ does a primitive root mod $n$ exist?
A: Iff $n = 1, 2, 4, p^k$, or $2 p^k$ where $p$ is an odd prime. So every prime modulus $p$ has a primitive root (Gauss). Non-examples: $n = 8, 12, 15, 16$ have no primitive root — their unit groups are NOT cyclic (products of smaller cyclics).

Q: Why does $(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$ fail to be cyclic?
A: $(\mathbb{Z}/8\mathbb{Z})^{\times} = \{1, 3, 5, 7\}$; every element squares to $1$ ($3^2 = 9 \equiv 1$, $5^2 = 25 \equiv 1$, $7^2 = 49 \equiv 1$). So the max order is $2$, not $4 = \varphi(8)$. The group is $(\mathbb{Z}/2\mathbb{Z})^2$, not $\mathbb{Z}/4\mathbb{Z}$ — two independent generators, not one.

## 8.5 Counting Primitive Roots

Q: How many primitive roots are there modulo $p$ (prime)?
A: Exactly $\varphi(p - 1)$. Proof: if $g$ is one primitive root, then $g^k$ is also a primitive root iff $\gcd(k, p-1) = 1$. Counting such $k$ in $\{1, \dots, p-1\}$ gives $\varphi(p-1)$. Example: mod $7$, $\varphi(6) = 2$ primitive roots; they are $3$ and $5$.

P: Find a primitive root modulo $7$.
S:
**IDENTIFY**: Test candidates against $\varphi(7) = 6$.

**PLAN**: For small $g$, check $g^d \not\equiv 1$ for proper divisors $d$ of $6$ (i.e., $d \in \{1, 2, 3\}$); then $\text{ord}_7(g) = 6$.

**EXECUTE**:
1. $g = 2$: $2^1 = 2, 2^2 = 4, 2^3 = 8 \equiv 1 \pmod 7$. Order $3$, NOT a primitive root.
2. $g = 3$: $3^1 = 3, 3^2 = 2, 3^3 = 6, 3^4 = 4, 3^5 = 5, 3^6 = 1 \pmod 7$. All six nonzero residues appear — order $6$, so $3$ IS a primitive root.

**EVALUATE**: Primitive roots are often small but must be verified. Once ONE is found, the others are $3^k$ for $\gcd(k, 6) = 1$, i.e., $k \in \{1, 5\}$: $3^1 = 3$ and $3^5 = 5$. Two primitive roots, matching $\varphi(6) = 2$.

## 8.6 Discrete Logarithms

C: Given a primitive root $g$ mod $n$ and an element $h \in (\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$, the [discrete logarithm] of $h$ to base $g$ is the unique $x \pmod{\varphi(n)}$ with $g^x \equiv h \pmod n$, written $\log_g h$.

Q: Why is the discrete log problem (DLP) believed to be hard?
A: Because no polynomial-time classical algorithm is known. Best classical algorithms (index calculus for $\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$) run in sub-exponential time $\exp((\log n)^{1/3} (\log \log n)^{2/3})$. For random primes $p$ of $2048$ bits, DLP is intractable in practice. Shor's quantum algorithm solves DLP in polynomial time — the same quantum threat that breaks RSA.

## 8.7 Diffie–Hellman Key Exchange

Q: How does Diffie–Hellman use primitive roots?
A: Public: a prime $p$ and primitive root $g$ mod $p$. Alice picks secret $a$, sends $A = g^a \bmod p$. Bob picks secret $b$, sends $B = g^b \bmod p$. Shared key: $K = B^a = A^b = g^{ab} \bmod p$. An eavesdropper sees $A, B$; recovering $K$ requires solving DLP. This was the first published public-key protocol (Diffie and Hellman, 1976).

Q: Why is Diffie–Hellman sometimes done in a subgroup rather than all of $\mathbb{F}_p^{\times}$?
A: Because DLP in a subgroup of large prime order $q \mid p-1$ is fast enough while keeping $\log p$ large for security — you hide structure in the prime-order subgroup. Modern implementations choose $p$ and $q$ so $q = (p-1)/2$ (Sophie Germain prime) or use elliptic curves where the group order is itself prime.

## 8.8 ElGamal Encryption

Q: How does ElGamal encryption use a primitive root?
A: Public: $(p, g, h = g^x \bmod p)$ with private $x$. To encrypt $m$: pick random $k$, compute $c_1 = g^k$ and $c_2 = m \cdot h^k \bmod p$. Decrypt: $m = c_2 \cdot (c_1^x)^{-1} \bmod p$. Security: distinguishing a valid ciphertext from random requires DLP-like assumptions. ElGamal is semantically secure under the DDH (Decisional Diffie–Hellman) assumption.

## 8.9 Baby-Step Giant-Step

Q: What is the [baby-step giant-step] algorithm for DLP?
A: To solve $g^x \equiv h \pmod n$ with $x \in [0, N)$: pick $m = \lceil \sqrt{N} \rceil$; write $x = im + j$ with $0 \leq i, j < m$. Precompute baby steps $h \cdot g^{-j}$ for $j = 0, \dots, m - 1$ and giant steps $g^{im}$ for $i = 0, \dots, m - 1$; find a match. Time and space $O(\sqrt{N})$ — much faster than brute force, worse than sub-exponential methods for large groups.

## 8.10 Pohlig–Hellman

Q: Why is it crucial that DLP groups have LARGE prime factors in the group order?
A: Because the [Pohlig–Hellman algorithm] solves DLP in groups of order $\prod p_i^{e_i}$ by reducing to DLPs in each subgroup of prime-power order. Total cost $O(\sum e_i \sqrt{p_i})$, dominated by the largest prime factor. If $p - 1$ has only small prime factors (smooth), DLP mod $p$ is easy — so cryptographers choose $p$ so $p - 1$ has at least one large prime factor.

## 8.11 Index of an Element

C: For a primitive root $g$ mod $n$, the [index] of $a$ (coprime to $n$) is $\text{ind}_g(a) = \log_g a \pmod{\varphi(n)}$.

Q: Why is index a multiplicative-to-additive translation?
A: Because $\text{ind}_g(ab) \equiv \text{ind}_g(a) + \text{ind}_g(b) \pmod{\varphi(n)}$: indices behave like logarithms, turning modular multiplication into addition mod $\varphi(n)$. Historically (pre-computers), indices were tabulated and used to do multiplication mod $p$ as addition — a shortcut analogous to slide rules.

## 8.12 The Structure Theorem

Q: How does CRT decompose $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$ for $n = \prod_i p_i^{e_i}$?
A: $(\mathbb{Z}/n\mathbb{Z})^{\times} \cong \prod_i (\mathbb{Z}/p_i^{e_i}\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$ — the unit group's structure reduces to the prime-power cases, giving a full decomposition into cyclic factors.

Q: What is the structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/p^k\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$ for an odd prime $p$?
A: Cyclic of order $p^{k-1}(p - 1)$.

Q: What is the structure of $(\mathbb{Z}/2^k\mathbb{Z})^{\times}$?
A: $\{e\}$ for $2^1$; $\mathbb{Z}/2$ for $2^2$; $\mathbb{Z}/2 \times \mathbb{Z}/2^{k-2}$ for $2^k$ with $k \geq 3$ (hence not cyclic for $k \geq 3$).
